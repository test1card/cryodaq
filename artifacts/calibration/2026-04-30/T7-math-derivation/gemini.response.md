### 4. Edge Cases

**When (־”T - ־”Tג‚€) approaches zero:**
If the measured temperature difference ־”T is very close to the baseline offset ־”Tג‚€ (which occurs when applied heating power P is extremely small, or the sample conductance G is exceptionally high), the denominator (־”T - ־”Tג‚€) approaches zero.

Physically, this means the measurement is dominated entirely by sensor noise and resolution limits; the temperature gradient generated by the heater is indistinguishable from the background offset, making the measurement of G invalid.

### 5. Discussion on the Independence Assumption

The initial derivation assumes that `u(־”T)` and `u(־”Tג‚€)` are uncorrelated independent variables. In a cryogenic experiment, this assumption requires careful scrutiny:

* **Why it might be realistic (Random effects):** ־”Tג‚€ is typically recorded in a steady state with the heater off (P=0), while ־”T is recorded later with the heater on (P>0). If the primary sources of uncertainty are random electrical noise, Johnson-Nyquist noise, or turbulent thermal fluctuations of the bath (Type A uncertainties), these noise profiles are independent because they are measured at two distinct points in time.
* **Why it might be flawed (Systematic effects):** Both ־”T and ־”Tג‚€ are typically read by the exact same physical sensors (e.g., the same pair of resistance thermometers or the same differential thermocouple) using the same digitizer hardware. Any systematic calibration error (Type B uncertainty)ג€”such as a stray thermal EMF in the cryostat wiring, amplifier drift, or a miscalibrated thermometer curveג€”will affect *both* measurements simultaneously and in the same direction. 
* **The impact of correlation:** If systematic errors strongly positively correlate ־”T and ־”Tג‚€, the covariance term `2 * (גˆ‚G/גˆ‚־”T) * (גˆ‚G/גˆ‚־”Tג‚€) * u(־”T, ־”Tג‚€)` must be subtracted from the GUM variance formula. Because גˆ‚G/גˆ‚־”T and גˆ‚G/גˆ‚־”Tג‚€ have opposite signs, a positive correlation actually *reduces* the overall uncertainty of the net difference `(־”T - ־”Tג‚€)`. Treating them as strictly independent can thus result in a conservative overestimate of the actual measurement uncertainty if common-mode errors exist.
